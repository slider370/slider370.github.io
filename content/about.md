I am **Khalid Ali**, a second-year Computer Engineering student at Georgia Tech. I chose this major because I enjoy understanding systems from the ground up: how logic, architecture, and implementation decisions shape what a device can do in the real world. I am especially motivated by work that connects theory to tangible outcomes, whether that means a simulation that finally passes, hardware that meets timing, or a project milestone that brings a design closer to deployment.

One of the most meaningful parts of my growth has been my involvement with **SiliconJackets**. The organization gives students exposure to realistic chip development workflows, and the structure is intentionally team-based. Members contribute across three core tracks:

- **Design**, where RTL and architecture choices are developed
- **Verification**, where test planning, simulation, and bug discovery ensure correctness
- **Physical Design**, where implementation constraints and backend flow turn logic into manufacturable hardware

Being around this process has improved how I approach engineering problems. I have learned to value reproducible workflows, clear interfaces between modules, and communication between teams with different priorities. In practice, that means documenting assumptions, being explicit about what has and has not been verified, and making decisions that balance progress with reliability.

I am currently building this same mindset through coursework and projects, including FPGA-based signal-processing work. I want to keep developing both technical depth and professional discipline so that I can contribute effectively on larger hardware teams.

Outside of classes and project milestones, I am focused on steady improvement: learning tools deeply enough to use them with confidence, asking better questions, and turning setbacks into clearer next steps. My goal is not just to complete assignments, but to build a strong foundation for long-term work in silicon engineering.
